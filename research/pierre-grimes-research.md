## 2. PHILOSOPHICAL MIDWIFERY: THE METHOD

### Origins and Foundation
- **Source Text**: Plato's *Theaetetus*, where Socrates describes his art as midwifery
- **Core Premise**: Socrates "assists in the delivery of men who are pregnant with either true ideas or false beliefs"
- **Developed by**: Pierre Grimes, Ph.D., beginning in the 1950s with Alan Watts
- **Institutional Home**: The Noetic Society, Inc., founded 1967 in Huntington Beach, CA (incorporated 1978)
- **Key Book**: *Philosophical Midwifery: A New Paradigm for Understanding Human Problems With its Validation* (with Dr. Regina Uliana, Hyparxis Press, 1998)

### Core Concepts

**The Logos** — The rational principle of the Self; the true, coherent understanding of one's nature. The Logos represents the "careful and intelligible mind" that constantly communicates for our benefit.

**The Pathologos** — A false belief about the Self that generates conflict, suffering, and dysfunction. The pathologos is the "sick logos" — an irrational belief masquerading as truth that has been accepted and integrated into one's worldview.

**The Movement**: Pathologos → Logos → Enlightenment Experience
"The movement from the pathologos to the logos and to enlightenment experiences proceeds along a well-defined route composed of a series of probing questions that uncover unsuspected false beliefs about the Self, and create the conditions for profound experiences akin to enlightenment experiences." (Grimes, APPA 2018)

### The Dialectic as Method
From Grimes' 1958 dissertation (*Dialectic as a Philosophical Method*) onward, he developed the dialectic as:
- A purely rational method of pursuing questions
- The application of knowledge that benefits the subject
- A mode of uncovering false beliefs, tracing them to their origins, and by understanding their roots and influence on one's life — deflating their influence

The method fulfills "the demands of the dialectic in being a method for providing **definitions, demonstrations, divisions, and analysis** in the quest for understanding the Self."

## 3. THE CROSS-EXAMINATION METHOD: STEP-BY-STEP STRUCTURE

Based on synthesis of the To Artemis program structure, APPA papers, Noetic Society descriptions, and lecture titles, the Grimes Dialectic follows this decision tree:

### PHASE I: ESTABLISHING THE TERRAIN

```
START
  |
  +-> 1. PRESENTING PROBLEM
  |     "What is the problem you're facing?"
  |     User states a current life problem, conflict, or goal
  |
  +-> 2. EXPECTATIONS
  |     "What do you expect to happen?"
  |     "What are your expectations about this situation?"
  |
  +-> 3. GOALS
  |     "What is your goal?"
  |     "What would excellence look like here?"
  |     -> Diagram the Goal (visual mapping)
  |
  +-> 4. GOAL-PROBLEM RELATIONSHIP
        "How does your goal relate to the problem?"
        "Is the problem blocking the goal?"
```

### PHASE II: SCENE ANALYSIS

```
  |
  +-> 5. STATEMENT OF THE PROBLEM
  |     Restate the problem precisely
  |
  +-> 6. SCENES
  |     "Describe the scenes where this problem occurs"
  |     "What happened? Who was involved?"
  |     -> Break problem into constituent scenes
  |
  +-> 7. SCENES WITHIN THE PROBLEM
  |     "Are there scenes within scenes?"
  |     "What sub-problems exist?"
  |
  +-> 8. THE DOORWAY INTO THE PROBLEM
  |     "What is the entry point to this problem?"
  |     "When did this pattern begin?"
  |
  +-> 9. OBSTACLES
  |     "What stands in your way?"
  |     "What are the obstacles to resolving this?"
  |
  +-> 10. INTERRELATIONSHIP AMONG PROBLEMS
        "How do your problems relate to each other?"
        "Is there a common thread?"
```

### PHASE III: ANALYSIS AND DIAGRAMMING

```
  |
  +-> 11. MAKING AN ANALYSIS
  |     Analyze the structure of the problem
  |     Identify patterns
  |
  +-> 12. DESCRIBING THE SCENES
  |     Detailed narrative of key scenes
  |
  +-> 13. A SINGLE SCENE
  |     Focus on one representative scene
  |     "What happened in this scene?"
  |
  +-> 14. THE HIGH AND THE LOW
  |     "What was the high point? The low point?"
  |     Identify the emotional arc
  |
  +-> 15. NEGATIVE STATE OF MIND
  |     "What negative state accompanies this?"
  |     This is the felt sense of the pathologos
  |
  +-> 16. DIAGRAMMING A PRESENT PROBLEM
  |     Visual diagram of the current problem structure
  |     (Example diagram provided as model)
  |
  +-> 17. DIAGRAMMING YOUR PRESENT PROBLEM
        Create your own diagram
```

### PHASE IV: CROSS-EXAMINATION (THE DIALECTIC PROPER)

This is where the Socratic cross-examination happens — probing the belief structure:

```
  |
  +-> 18. IDENTIFY THE FALSE BELIEF (PATHOLOGOS)
  |     "What belief are you holding here?"
  |     "Is this belief true?"
  |     "Can you see how this belief is functioning?"
  |
  +-> 19. TRACE TO ORIGIN
  |     "When did you first accept this belief?"
  |     "Where did this belief come from?"
  |     "What scene/experience created it?"
  |
  +-> 20. EXAMINE THE BELIEF
  |     "Is this belief consistent with the Self/Logos?"
  |     "Does this belief lead to excellence or conflict?"
  |     "What would it mean if this belief were false?"
  |
  +-> 21. TEST CONTRADICTIONS
  |     "Is there a contradiction in this belief?"
  |     "Does holding this belief conflict with your goal?"
  |     "Is the opposite also sometimes true?"
  |
  +-> 22. DEFLATE THE BELIEF
        Through understanding -> the belief loses power
        "Now that you see the origin and function of this belief,
         does it still hold power over you?"
```

### PHASE V: INTEGRATION AND REFLECTION

```
  |
  +-> 23. REFLECTION ON THE PROBLEM
  |     Step back and review what was discovered
  |
  +-> 24. TIME TO REFLECT
  |     Journal entries; quiet consideration
  |
  +-> 25. RECOGNITION OF THE LOGOS
  |     "What does the rational Self understand now?"
  |     "What insight has emerged?"
  |
  +-> 26. EXCELLENCE
  |     "What does excellence look like in this situation?"
  |     "How does the Self express itself here?"
  |
  +-> 27. RESOLUTION / CONTINUATION
        Either: Problem resolved -> New goal or new problem
        Or: Deeper layer discovered -> Return to Phase I/II
        Or: Ready to Quit? -> Option to pause
```

## 4. DECISION POINTS / BRANCHING LOGIC

The To Artemis program and the live midwifery sessions include several key decision points:

```
DECISION TREE BRANCHES:

At "Statement of the Problem":
  +-> Problem is clear -> Proceed to Scene Analysis
  +-> Problem is unclear -> Re-examine Goals first

At "Scenes":
  +-> Multiple scenes -> Analyze interrelationships
  +-> Single scene -> Focus on one scene deeply

At "Obstacles":
  +-> External obstacles -> Examine beliefs about constraints
  +-> Internal obstacles -> This IS the pathologos; go deeper

At "Negative State of Mind":
  +-> State is recognized -> Trace to origin belief
  +-> State is unclear -> Return to scene description

At "Ready to Quit?":
  +-> Yes -> Journal reflection; return later
  +-> No -> Continue analysis

At belief examination:
  +-> Belief deflated -> Integration/Reflection
  +-> Belief resists -> Dig deeper into origin scene
  +-> Deeper belief found -> Recurse (new pathologos discovered)

At "Excellence":
  +-> Clear vision -> Resolution
  +-> Still unclear -> Return to Goals with new understanding
```

## 5. DREAMWORK METHOD (RELATED)

Grimes also applies the same dialectic to dream exploration (NSDRM sessions):
- Dreams are communications from the "Dream Master" (the intelligible mind)
- The same cross-examination structure applies
- Dream symbols are treated as scenes to be analyzed
- The dreamer's false beliefs are revealed through the dream narrative
- Same movement: Dream -> Scene Analysis -> Pathologos Identification -> Deflation -> Insight

## 6. THE FOURTEEN STEPS (From Lecture)

From the 2018 lecture "The Philosophy of the Self, In Fourteen Steps" (2018-06-27 NSPRM), Grimes outlined 14 steps in the Parmenides that define the Logos. These map the structure of the Self through Proclus' propositions:
- Each step examines a relation (self-to-self, self-to-others, others-to-self, others-to-others)
- Each relation is examined three ways: what is true, what is not true, what is both
- This generates the logical framework for the cross-examination

## 8. ANALYSIS: WHAT MAKES THE METHOD DISTINCTIVE

1. **Not traditional therapy**: It's purely rational/philosophical, not psychological
2. **Pathologos concept**: The specific idea of a "false belief about the Self" as the root of problems is unique to Grimes
3. **Diagramming**: Visual mapping of problems is integral to the process
4. **Scene-based**: Problems are broken into concrete scenes, not abstract analysis
5. **Origin-tracing**: Every false belief is traced to its origin scene
6. **Recursive**: The method is fractal — each discovery may reveal deeper layers
7. **Goal-oriented**: Always connected to excellence and the vision of the Self
8. **Dream integration**: Dreams are treated as equally valid entry points
9. **Dialectical rigor**: Follows Platonic/Proclean dialectic structure (definitions, demonstrations, divisions, analysis)
10. **Self-validating**: The method claims to demonstrate its own truth through application
